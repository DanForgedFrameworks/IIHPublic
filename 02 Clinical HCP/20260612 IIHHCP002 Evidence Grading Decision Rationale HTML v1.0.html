IIHHCP002 — Evidence Grading Decision Rationale

Relates to: IIHHCP002 — 2018 Consensus Guidelines Interactive Tool (HTML v1.0)  ·  Prepared: 12 June 2026  ·  Document version: v1.0
For clinical review team — evaluation requested

1. Purpose of this document

This is a decision record for the IIH UK clinical review team. It explains a deliberate departure from the original build brief for the IIHHCP002 interactive tool, so that reviewers can evaluate whether the chosen approach is clinically and editorially sound before the asset is signed off.

It concerns one question only: how the strength / evidence of each recommendation is presented in the tool.

2. The brief versus the source

The build brief specified that each recommendation should be shown with its “evidence grade (A/B/C/GPP as in the paper)”.

The source paper does not contain A/B/C/GPP evidence grades. The authoritative source — Mollan SP, Davies B, Silver NC, et al. Idiopathic intracranial hypertension: consensus guidelines on management. J Neurol Neurosurg Psychiatry 2018;89:1088–1100 (IIHREF001) — is a UK Delphi consensus statement, not a GRADE-style evidence-graded guideline. No A/B/C/GPP scheme appears anywhere in the publication.

What the paper actually is

  • A specialist interest group (SIG) plus a wider Delphi panel produced guidance statements; all statements obtained ≥75% consensus.
  • It was reviewed by international experts and four professional bodies (ABN, BASH, RCOphth, SBNS).
  • The authors are explicit that the evidence base is weak for most statements.
“We recognise that we were limited by the lack of high-quality evidence for the majority of the statements made … These statements are not mandatory recommendations but are intended to be used as a guide for doctors who investigate and treat IIH.” — Mollan et al., 2018, Closing statement.

3. Why we did not assign A/B/C/GPP grades

Assigning letter grades that the original authors never published would mean inventing an evidence hierarchy and attributing it to a peer-reviewed consensus document. On a tool intended for practising clinicians (neurologists, ophthalmologists, neurosurgeons, orthoptists, allied health), that carries a real risk of:

  • implying a level of evidential certainty that does not exist;
  • misrepresenting the source and undermining the tool’s credibility on clinical review;
  • creating a false distinction between statements that the authors treated as a single consensus tier.

This would be an editorial fabrication, not a faithful reproduction of the guideline. We judged that unacceptable for a clinical reference asset.

4. What the tool shows instead

Each recommendation in IIHHCP002 carries three faithful, source-derived elements in place of a fabricated grade:

  1. A “Strength” label drawn from the guideline’s own directive language. The 2018 statements consistently use modal verbs to signal how firmly they are made; we surface that wording directly.
  2. An “Evidence base” line naming the actual underlying evidence for that statement (randomised trial, observational / case-series, physiological, or consensus / expert opinion).
  3. The authors’ own “Uncertainty” note, reproduced where the paper states one.

Strength labels and how they map to the source

Label in toolDerived from guideline wordingMeaning
Must / Mandatory“must”, “mandatory”Stated as a requirement (e.g. BP measurement; venography within 24h).
Should / Recommended“should”The consensus position for usual practice.
May / Could“could”, “may”, “there may be a role”An option in selected patients; clinician judgement applies.
Not recommended“not recommended”, “is not … established / a treatment”The guideline advises against, generally or routinely.
Where genuine trial evidence exists, it is named. The clearest example is acetazolamide, where the tool’s Evidence line cites the IIH Treatment Trial (Wall et al. 2014) and the 2015 Cochrane review — distinguishing it from the largely observational basis of the surgical statements. This gives reviewers and clinicians a truthful evidence signal that letter grades would have flattened.

5. Transparency within the tool

The tool does not hide this. A collapsible “How to read these recommendations — evidence and strength” panel on the tool’s main screen states plainly that the 2018 guideline is a Delphi consensus document with no formal evidence grades, and explains that the strength labels reflect the guideline’s wording rather than a graded hierarchy. This decision record is linked from that panel.

6. What we are asking the review team to evaluate

Review questions

  1. Do you agree that A/B/C/GPP grades should not be fabricated for a consensus source that does not contain them?
  2. Is the strength-from-wording scheme (Must / Should / May / Not recommended) an acceptable and accurate representation of the 2018 statements?
  3. Are the per-recommendation Evidence-base descriptions accurate and appropriately cautious?
  4. Is the in-tool methodology disclosure sufficiently clear for a clinical audience?
  5. If a formal graded version is later desired, should it use a recognised framework (e.g. GRADE) applied transparently and labelled as an IIH UK overlay — not attributed to the 2018 authors?

Please return comments to the asset lead. Any agreed change is a content edit to cg-content.js only; the tool rebuilds from that single source.
